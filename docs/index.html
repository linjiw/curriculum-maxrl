Research notes · curriculum learning × MaxRL

A teacher curriculum derived from the MaxRL objective's own algebra

Maximum Likelihood RL (MaxRL) reweights gradients toward hard prompts — an implicit curriculum. But it goes silent exactly where a curriculum matters most: prompts whose rollout groups all fail. We show the fix falls out of the estimator's own math: the expected learning signal a prompt receives is exactly 2·(pass@N − pass@1), and sampling prompts by that quantity is the curriculum. This page explains how MaxRL works, what we propose, and what we verified.

1 · How MaxRL works, in three steps

Step 1 — RL and maximum likelihood optimize different things. For a prompt x, let p be the model's probability of producing a verified-correct answer (its pass rate). Standard RL maximizes the expected reward E[p]; maximum likelihood maximizes E[log p]. Their gradients differ by one factor:

∇JRL = 𝔼x[ ∇p ]    vs.    ∇JML = 𝔼x[ 1p ∇p ]

That 1/p is the whole story: maximum likelihood pours learning signal into hard, low-pass-rate prompts, while plain RL treats every prompt equally. In differentiable supervised learning this is why cross-entropy works so well — but with sampled rollouts and a binary verifier you cannot compute ∇log p directly.

Step 2 — the Maclaurin bridge. The paper's key identity expands log p in failure events, turning the intractable objective into an infinite mixture of pass@k objectives:

log p = −Σk≥1 (1−p)kk   ⇒   ∇JML = Σk≥1 1k ∇pass@k

Plain RL keeps only the k=1 term — it is a first-order approximation of maximum likelihood. Truncating the series at order T gives a family of objectives that interpolates from RL (T=1) to ML (T→∞).

Step 3 — a one-line estimator. Sample N rollouts, count K successes. REINFORCE averages score functions over all N samples; MaxRL averages over the K successful ones (and skips the prompt when K=0). Theorem 2 of the paper: this simple change is unbiased for the truncated objective with T = N. So extra rollouts don't just cut variance — they raise the fidelity of the objective itself. In the practical algorithm it is literally one line: normalize the advantage by the group's mean reward (instead of the std as in GRPO, or nothing as in RLOO).

2 · Where the implicit curriculum goes silent

The weight-function view is a population (infinite-sample) statement. With finite groups of N rollouts, three gaps open up — and they are precisely the gaps a data-level teacher can close:

Dead prompts (K = 0)

If p ≪ 1/N, all N rollouts fail with probability (1−p)N ≈ 1. The group is dropped: zero gradient, wasted generation compute. No choice of w(p) can put signal where the group dies. In our maze experiments, uniform sampling wasted ~65% of all rollout groups this way.

Mastered prompts (p → 1)

∇p → 0, so rollouts are spent re-confirming what the model knows. GRPO is actively worse here: its w(p) inverts and upweights easy prompts, sharpening the distribution.

Uniform rollout budgets

The paper uses a fixed N per prompt. But since the truncation order is tied to the group size (T = N), harder prompts would benefit from larger groups twice over: more chance of a first success, and a higher-order approximation of the ML objective.

A striking finite-sample fact we verified: at p = 0.01, N = 32, GRPO's population curve promises weight ≈ 0.199, but the realized expected signal is 0.100 — half — because 72% of groups are all-fail and contribute nothing. Population weight curves describe a limit no finite-group trainer lives in.

3 · Our proposal: read the curriculum off the estimator

Instead of importing a curriculum heuristic (difficulty bands, learning-progress bandits), we asked: how much learning signal does the MaxRL estimator actually emit on a prompt, in expectation? Define the advantage mass of a group as the total magnitude of its advantage weights, Σ|w|. For MaxRL's variance-reduced estimator (wsucc = 1/K − 1/N, wfail = −1/N, group dropped at K=0), the algebra collapses to something exact and interpretable:

𝔼[ Σ|w| ] = 2·( pass@N(p) − pass@1(p) ) = 2·( (1−(1−p)N) − p )

The expected learning signal on a prompt is twice the probability that it is solvable within N attempts but not within one. That is a compute-indexed formalization of the zone of proximal development, produced by the estimator itself. It vanishes at both ends — beyond-frontier prompts (group dies) and mastered prompts (no headroom) — and peaks at

p★ = 1 − N−1/(N−1) ≈ ln NN

so larger group sizes automatically shift the optimal curriculum band toward harder prompts — the teacher and the objective share the same compute knob.

The teacher loop

  1. Track. Keep a decayed Beta(α, β) posterior over each prompt's pass rate, updated from every group's observed successes (decay 0.7 — validated below: the posterior's job is tracking the moving student, not remembering).
  2. Score. Thompson-sample p̃ from each posterior; compute u(p̃) = (1−(1−p̃)N) − p̃. Sampling from the posterior gives optimism on rarely-visited prompts for free.
  3. Sample. Draw the next batch of prompts ∝ uγ (γ≈4 when tasks share skills, 1 on flat pools — see the compounding result below), mixed with a uniform floor (default 10%) — the floor is the anti-forgetting/replay channel and keeps posteriors fresh.
  4. Allocate (optional). Split the rollout budget by greedy water-filling on the marginal mass p̃(1−p̃)N — the probability the next rollout is the group's first success. Total mass is concave in each Ni, so greedy is optimal.
  5. Train. MaxRL advantages, unchanged — the estimator was never modified, so all of the paper's unbiasedness results carry over untouched.

In the repo this is FrontierTeacher + CurriculumSampler: ~150 lines, wired into the official MaxRL/verl trainer behind +data.curriculum.enable=true, with teacher state checkpointed alongside the model — and packaged environment-agnostically in frontier_rl/ for gym and robotics tasks.

The two-line proof (Proposition 1)

Condition on the number of successes K. For K ≥ 1, the MaxRL weights are wsucc = 1/K − 1/N (K of them) and wfail = −1/N (N−K of them), so the total magnitude telescopes:

Σ|w| = K(1K − 1N) + N−KN = 2(1 − KN)   (and 0 when K=0)

Taking expectations with K ~ Binomial(N, p):

𝔼[Σ|w|] = 2·(P(K≥1) − 𝔼[K]/N) = 2·((1−(1−p)N) − p) = 2·(pass@N − pass@1) ∎

Every claim on this page traces to identities of this kind — seven propositions with proofs in PROOFS.md (peak location, water-filling optimality, RLOO ≡ learnability, the ~N× frontier-signal ratio, the hindsight conditional-law characterization), each Monte-Carlo verified in run_validation.py.

Every teacher/hindsight variant beats both uniform baselines on learning speed at equal wall-clock. The most instructive result is a refuted hypothesis: we predicted a frontier teacher would rescue GRPO from pass@k collapse by retiring the mastered prompts its inverted w(p) overweights. The opposite happened — frontier+grpo collapsed harder (pass@8 0.332→0.269) than uniform+grpo (0.351→0.312), losing easy-level retention, while MaxRL under the same teacher kept pass@8 flat-to-up. GRPO's inversion was quietly maintaining easy prompts; a curriculum removes that maintenance. So the collapse is an objective-level pathology that data-level curricula amplify rather than fix — a frontier curriculum requires likelihood-style weighting to be safe, which strengthens the paper's central claim from the outside.

Multi-seed confirmation (seeds 0–2, paired via shared per-seed warmstarts): both teacher variants beat uniform+maxrl on AUC in every seed (6/6 paired deltas positive); frontier_alp+maxrl wins final mean-pass with the tightest spread (0.246 ± 0.002 vs 0.230 ± 0.015). The coverage divergence is systematic, not seed noise: GRPO's pass@8 decays in every seed (mean 0.308→0.271) while teacher+MaxRL grows it (0.316→0.348) — the paper's coverage-collapse dynamic reproduced at 1.26M scale, with the curriculum widening the gap in both directions.

Hindsight relabeling — the biggest single gain

MaxRL's Theorem 1 makes the estimator success-conditioned: it learns only from successes. The HER-style complement: a failed trajectory is a success for the goal it actually reached. Relabeling dead groups to their deepest achieved sub-goal converts wasted compute into live MaxRL groups:

Division of labor: the teacher avoids spending compute beyond the frontier; hindsight salvages whatever still fails there. In the hard 16-level regime hindsight alone (0.970) even beats the teacher alone (0.961), and the combination stays best (0.978). Two later validation results sharpened this: the hindsight gradient is exact on-structure — per-group cosine to the true success-conditioned ML gradient equals that of unbiased fresh groups (0.956 vs 0.958), and the mean relabeled gradient converges to cosine 1.000 (Prop. 6 + V1); and in a frontier-heavy regime (max pool pass rate 10⁻⁵) uniform, DAPO dynamic sampling, and the plain teacher all flatline at exactly 0 while teacher+hindsight reaches 0.98 — reallocating compute among unlearnable tasks cannot help; hindsight creates the missing signal by inventing the curriculum below the pool, igniting learnability within ~400 groups, then going silent as natural successes take over.

Beating the oracle — and the concentration surprise

Two late results sharpened the method. First, sampling ∝ u(p)γ with γ≈4 beats proportional sampling (AUC 0.782 vs 0.728): the mass functional is the right ordering, but learning compounds on structured task pools — steps on the highest-mass task unlock the next — so the optimal temperature is sharper than proportional. (A two-line ODE model isolates the cause: with shared skills the γ effect reproduces quantitatively; on a flat pool it vanishes.) Second, the headline:

On the GPU maze, dense hindsight (relabel every deep-enough failed rollout, ~16 relabels/step) is likewise the new champion (final 0.258, best 0.269 at matched wall-clock), while routing relabeled successes into the teacher's posterior inflated it (p̂ 0.81 vs true 0.47) and was dropped — the posterior should see only requested-task evidence.

Hindsight gradients are exact, not approximate

The load-bearing question for hindsight: is a relabeled gradient a real ML gradient or plausible noise? On the skill chain we can compute the true success-conditioned ML gradient exactly and measure alignment. Result — relabeled (hindsight) groups are exactly as well-aligned as unbiased fresh groups sampled directly on the relabeled task, and the mean hindsight gradient converges to the truth:

5 · One schedule, any environment: frontier_rl

The validated schedule is packaged as an environment-agnostic framework (frontier_rl/, numpy-only core). You implement four methods — task bins, group rollout, hindsight relabel, and one weighted policy update — and the teacher, estimator, and hindsight logic come for free. 6 · Insights we took from the paper

Compute changes the objective, not just the variance

For REINFORCE, more rollouts = lower variance on the same objective. For MaxRL, more rollouts = a strictly better objective (T = N). Any curriculum that reallocates rollouts is therefore also reallocating objective fidelity — which is why rollout allocation deserves to be a first-class curriculum axis.

The zero-gradient zone is structural, not a bug

Dropping K=0 groups is what makes the estimator unbiased for the truncated objective. So MaxRL cannot be patched at the loss level to learn from all-fail prompts — the fix has to live in the sampling distribution. That is the cleanest argument we know for pairing it with a teacher.

Population curves mislead at finite N

The elegant w(p) table is an infinite-sample statement. Realized signal is throttled by pass@N for every estimator; on hard prompts GRPO delivers half of what its curve promises. Curriculum design should target the finite-sample quantity — which is exactly what advantage mass is.

Existing curricula are special cases

SFL's learnability p(1−p) is our utility at N=1 and RLOO's exact advantage mass; SEC's empirical-|advantage| bandit reward has our formulas as its exact expectation. The advantage-mass teacher is “oracle SEC for MaxRL” — same target, computable from a posterior before a prompt is ever visited.
