Research notes · curriculum learning × MaxRL

The estimator is the curriculum

In RL with verifiable rewards, rollout compute is wasted twice: on prompts the model has mastered (nothing left to learn) and on prompts it can't touch (every rollout fails — group estimators emit zero gradient). We measured it: under uniform sampling, historical logs showed 65–75% zero-weight groups, but the old counter mixed all-fail and all-pass groups. The fix needs no new machinery. We prove practical MaxRL's expected scalar coefficient mass is exactly 2·(pass@N − pass@1) — twice the probability of being solvable in N tries but not in one — and that formula supplies a curriculum proxy: sample by it to favor groups likely to emit practical MaxRL coefficient mass. An already-realized all-fail group has no practical MaxRL update, so selection alone cannot recover that group; where a verifier-valid goal rewrite exists, we recycle a failed rollout as a success for an achieved goal. Together they beat a true-pass-rate proportional-priority baseline on the toy — because that baseline only reallocates original-task groups, while recycling adds verifier-valid auxiliary targets.

The one-sentence pitch

Read the curriculum off the estimator's own algebra, and salvage what the estimator throws away. No difficulty-band hyperparameters — the rollout budget N you already chose sets the band (peak difficulty ≈ ln N / N); the practical drop-both estimator targets order N−1; raw and always-retained-CV variants retain the paper's order-N result.

0 · Why this direction

MaxRL's weight-function view (below) shows the likelihood objective already pours gradient into hard prompts — an implicit curriculum. That raises a question with a non-obvious answer: if the objective already reweights toward hard prompts, is there anything left for a curriculum to do? Either curricula are redundant with likelihood-based RL (a useful negative result), or they do something gradient reweighting cannot (a useful positive one).

The answer is sharply the second, for a structural reason: weights act on prompts only after they are sampled, and only when at least one rollout succeeds. However w(p) is shaped, it cannot rescue a prompt whose group came back all-fail — the group is dropped in the practical implementation—and it cannot un-spend compute burned re-confirming mastered prompts. So the curriculum's real job is not "emphasize hard prompts" (the objective does that); it is to manage exactly the two regimes the estimator is blind to. That reframing — the estimator's blind spots define the curriculum's job description — is the origin of everything on this page.

Channel 1 → the teacher (waste avoidance)

Theorem 1 expresses the ML gradient through success-conditioned scores; the practical centered update also scores failures in live mixed groups but discards K=0 groups. Its expected signal per prompt has a closed form — and that form, 2·(pass@N − pass@1), is a zone-of-proximal-development functional the estimator was computing implicitly all along. Sampling by it is the curriculum. Bounded by the oracle ceiling: allocation only redistributes signal that exists.

Channel 2 → the recycler (signal creation)

If an all-fail group supplies no update, create a verified auxiliary success. A failed rollout is a verified success for the goal it actually reached; relabeling dead groups yields verifier-valid auxiliary gradients. Equality to a fresh target update needs the law/moment conditions in Proposition 6. This changes what a sampled failure can yield in a way prompt selection alone cannot.

There is a third, silent channel: objective safety. The identical teacher grew coverage under MaxRL in every seed and amplified GRPO's collapse in every seed — the objective underneath decides whether a curriculum is safe at all (section 4). Together: the teacher allocates, hindsight creates, the objective decides whether either is safe. The full evidence decomposition, regime map, and practitioner playbook are in EVIDENCE.md.

1 · How MaxRL works, in three steps

Step 1 — RL and maximum likelihood optimize different things. For a prompt x, let p be the model's probability of producing a verified-correct answer (its pass rate). Standard RL maximizes the expected reward E[p]; maximum likelihood maximizes E[log p]. Their gradients differ by one factor:

∇JRL = 𝔼x[ ∇p ]    vs.    ∇JML = 𝔼x[ 1p ∇p ]

That 1/p is the whole story: maximum likelihood pours learning signal into hard, low-pass-rate prompts, while plain RL treats every prompt equally. In differentiable supervised learning this is why cross-entropy works so well — but with sampled rollouts and a binary verifier you cannot compute ∇log p directly.

Step 2 — the Maclaurin bridge. The paper's key identity expands log p in failure events, turning the intractable objective into an infinite mixture of pass@k objectives:

log p = −Σk≥1 (1−p)kk   ⇒   ∇JML = Σk≥1 1k ∇pass@k

Plain RL keeps only the k=1 term — it is a first-order approximation of maximum likelihood. Truncating the series at order T gives a family of objectives that interpolates from RL (T=1) to ML (T→∞).

Step 3 — a one-line estimator. Sample N rollouts, count K successes. REINFORCE averages score functions over all N samples; MaxRL averages over the K successful ones (and skips the prompt when K=0). Theorem 2 of the paper: this simple change is unbiased for the truncated objective with T = N. The always-retained control-variate form is also order N, while practical Algorithm 1 drops both terms at K=0 and targets T = N−1. In the practical algorithm it is one line: normalize the advantage by the group's mean reward (instead of the std as in GRPO, or nothing as in RLOO).

2 · Where the implicit curriculum goes silent

The weight-function view is a population (infinite-sample) statement. With finite groups of N rollouts, three gaps open up — and they are precisely the gaps a data-level teacher can close:

Dead prompts (K = 0)

If p ≪ 1/N, all N rollouts fail with probability (1−p)N ≈ 1. The group is dropped: zero gradient, wasted generation compute. No choice of w(p) can put signal where the group dies. Historical maze logs recorded about 65% zero-weight groups under uniform sampling, but that counter mixed K=0 with K=N. It therefore cannot estimate all-fail waste; corrected runs log those cases separately.

Mastered prompts (p → 1)

∇p → 0, so rollouts are spent re-confirming what the model knows. GRPO is actively worse here: its w(p) inverts and upweights easy prompts, sharpening the distribution.

Uniform rollout budgets

The paper uses a fixed N per prompt. But since the truncation order is tied to the group size (T = N), harder prompts would benefit from larger groups twice over: more chance of a first success, and a higher-order approximation of the ML objective.

A striking finite-sample fact we verified: at p = 0.01, N = 32, GRPO's population curve promises weight ≈ 0.199, but the realized expected signal is 0.100 — half — because 72% of groups are all-fail and contribute nothing. Population weight curves describe a limit no finite-group trainer lives in.

3 · Our proposal: read the curriculum off the estimator

Instead of importing a curriculum heuristic (difficulty bands, learning-progress bandits), we asked: how much scalar coefficient mass does practical MaxRL emit on a prompt, in expectation? Define the advantage mass of a group as the total magnitude of its advantage weights, Σ|w|. For MaxRL's variance-reduced estimator (wsucc = 1/K − 1/N, wfail = −1/N, group dropped at K=0), the algebra collapses to something exact and interpretable:

𝔼[ Σ|w| ] = 2·( pass@N(p) − pass@1(p) ) = 2·( (1−(1−p)N) − p )

The expected scalar coefficient mass on a prompt is twice the probability that it is solvable within N attempts but not within one. That is a compute-indexed formalization of the zone of proximal development, produced by the estimator itself. It vanishes at both ends — beyond-frontier prompts (group dies) and mastered prompts (no headroom) — and peaks at

p★ = 1 − N−1/(N−1) ≈ ln NN

so larger group sizes shift the coefficient-mass peak toward harder prompts. This proxy is not the gradient norm or a proof of optimal learning progress.

Watch it work: the frontier walk, animated

The teacher loop

  1. Track. Keep discounted Beta(α, β) pseudo-counts for each prompt's pass rate, updated from every group's observed successes (decay 0.7—the estimate's job is tracking the moving student, not remembering).
  2. Score. Draw p̃ from each pseudo-count distribution; compute u(p̃) = (1−(1−p̃)N) − p̃. Randomized draws encourage exploration of rarely-visited prompts for free.
  3. Sample. Draw the next batch of prompts ∝ uγ (γ≈4 when tasks share skills, 1 on flat pools — see the compounding result below), mixed with a uniform floor (default 10%)—the floor is the anti-forgetting/replay channel and guarantees revisits.
  4. Allocate (optional). Split the rollout budget by greedy water-filling on the marginal mass p̃(1−p̃)N — the probability the next rollout is the group's first success. For fixed supplied pass rates, feasible integer bounds, and a one-step budget, separable concavity makes greedy exact for this proxy.
  5. Train. Practical MaxRL advantages, unchanged within a selected task. Curriculum sampling changes the task distribution, and the drop-both estimator has effective order N−1.

In the repo this is FrontierTeacher + CurriculumSampler: ~150 lines, wired into the official MaxRL/verl trainer behind +data.curriculum.enable=true, with teacher state checkpointed alongside the model — and packaged environment-agnostically in frontier_rl/ for gym and robotics tasks.

The two-line proof (Proposition 1)

Condition on the number of successes K. For K ≥ 1, the MaxRL weights are wsucc = 1/K − 1/N (K of them) and wfail = −1/N (N−K of them), so the total magnitude telescopes:

Σ|w| = K(1K − 1N) + N−KN = 2(1 − KN)   (and 0 when K=0)

Taking expectations with K ~ Binomial(N, p):

𝔼[Σ|w|] = 2·(P(K≥1) − 𝔼[K]/N) = 2·((1−(1−p)N) − p) = 2·(pass@N − pass@1) ∎

The mathematical claims on this page trace to seven propositions with proofs in PROOFS.md (peak location, fixed-p water-filling, RLOO mass ∝ learnability, the frontier mass ratio, the hindsight conditional-law characterization). Core estimator and allocation identities have exact-enumeration/unit tests; experiment-specific checks live in run_validation.py.

Hindsight relabeling — the biggest single gain

MaxRL's Theorem 1 expresses the ML gradient through a success-conditioned score. The practical centered estimator uses failures in live mixed groups, but discards all-fail groups. The HER-style complement: a failed trajectory is a success for the goal it actually reached. Relabeling dead groups to their deepest achieved sub-goal converts wasted compute into live MaxRL groups:

Division of labor: the teacher avoids spending compute beyond the frontier; hindsight salvages whatever still fails there. An archived 16-level sweep (driver/output not retained) reported that hindsight partially substituted for the teacher; those particular numbers are not current evidence. Two later validation results sharpened this: centered hindsight is direction-aligned but scale-biased, while success-only has unit scale under the special chain law (Prop. 6 + V1); and in a frontier-heavy regime (max pool pass rate 10⁻⁵) uniform, DAPO dynamic sampling, and the plain teacher all flatline at exactly 0 while teacher+hindsight reaches 0.98 — reallocating compute among unlearnable tasks cannot help; hindsight creates the missing signal by inventing the curriculum below the pool, igniting learnability within ~400 groups, then going silent as natural successes take over.

Two late results sharpened the method. The hidden benefits — quieter, arguably worth more:

Zero difficulty hyperparameters

ADARFT-style bands need targets and widths tuned per task. Here the band is derived: the rollout budget N you already chose sets the peak (≈ ln N / N) and the width. Doubling N moves the curriculum harder automatically — the compute knob and the curriculum knob are the same knob.

A safety diagnosis, not just a method

The historical maze evidence raises a testable warning: if GRPO relies on easy prompts for maintenance, a frontier curriculum may amplify coverage loss. The current three-seed audited result is hypothesis-generating; a corrected factorial is required before treating this as a safety rule.

Conceptual compression

Learnability curricula (p(1−p)), DAPO-style dynamic filtering, and HER stop being three separate tricks: their coefficient-mass scores meet at N=2, filtering changes sampling, and HER adds auxiliary success targets. Fewer moving parts to reason about, without claiming the methods are identical.

Free telemetry

The teacher's pseudo-count estimate is a live difficulty map of the task pool—mastered/frontier/dead fractions per step. Historical GPU diagnostics tracked held-out pass rates, but corrected validation is still required.

7 · Insights we took from the paper

Compute changes the objective, not just the variance

For REINFORCE, more rollouts lower variance on the same objective. For raw/always-CV MaxRL, N sets order N; for practical drop-both MaxRL it sets order N−1. Rollout allocation therefore changes both signal probability and effective objective order.

The zero-gradient zone is structural, not a bug

The raw success-average estimator is order-N unbiased. Dropping the centered control variate at K=0 creates practical order N−1 and a zero update on all-fail groups. A teacher or semantically valid auxiliary target addresses that practical dead zone.

Population curves mislead at finite N

The elegant w(p) table is an infinite-sample statement. Realized signal is throttled by pass@N for every estimator; on hard prompts GRPO delivers half of what its curve promises. Curriculum design should target the finite-sample quantity — which is exactly what advantage mass is.

Existing curricula are special cases

SFL's learnability p(1−p) equals our half-mass utility at N=2 and half RLOO's expected coefficient mass. SEC's empirical-|advantage| reward has these formulas as expectations. Our pseudo-count teacher is a model-based estimate of expected SEC-style mass, not an oracle.
